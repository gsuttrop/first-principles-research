Background

I'm a first-year IBA student at Rotterdam School of Management, pursuing a parallel degree in Philosophy at Erasmus School of Philosophy. My interest in markets started with a simple question: how do you know what something is worth?

This platform started as a project to apply valuation frameworks in practice, DCF models, scenario analysis, WACC derivation, to companies where the investment case sits at the intersection of macro and micro. Alongside this, I work as a working student at OK Asset, the private real estate arm of Kitzig Interior Design, evaluating property acquisitions through cash flow analysis, rental yield calculations, and debt service assessments. That work has sharpened my understanding of how financial structure translates into real return outcomes.

The question that drives most of my thinking is this: will AI and the next technology wave concentrate power further in existing industrial centres, or will it be the first technology that genuinely allows emerging markets to leapfrog? That is not a stock-picking question. It is a macro-sociological thesis that needs a company as its proof of concept. Both BYD and Telkom sit at exactly that inflection point: one as a case study in how China's industrial strategy has built a globally dominant cost structure, the other as a bet on whether Southeast Asian digital infrastructure can compound independently of Western tech platforms.

I am drawn to companies where macro explains why now and fundamentals explain what it is worth, equal weight to both. Infrastructure, emerging market digital economies, and the geopolitics of industrial policy are the areas I keep returning to. I am still early in building a framework for all of this, but that is precisely the point of doing it in public.

Methodology

Every analysis follows a consistent, transparent process: build the model from scratch, stress-test the assumptions, derive a view on intrinsic value.

DCF / FCFF
Free cash flow projections over a 5-year horizon, discounted at WACC. Terminal value estimated via exit multiple method.
WACC via CAPM
Cost of equity derived from CAPM. Beta sourced from comparable companies. Cost of debt from public filings. Weighted by capital structure.
Scenario Analysis
Bull, base, and bear cases driven by macro assumptions, competitive dynamics, and execution risk with explicit probability weights.
Industry Analysis
Competitive positioning, market structure, and macro-driver analysis used to frame and validate financial model assumptions.
Philosophy

Good investment analysis is closer to philosophy than mathematics. The numbers matter, but the framework matters more — which assumptions actually drive the value, where the market is likely wrong, and what would have to be true for the thesis to fail. My parallel study of analytical and political philosophy has sharpened how I think about uncertainty, argumentation, and the limits of knowledge — all of which apply directly to financial analysis.

Executive Summary

BYD is the world's largest New Energy Vehicle manufacturer by volume and one of the few global OEMs with fully integrated battery production, power electronics, and vehicle assembly. The company's structural advantages — including proprietary Blade Battery technology and a negative cash conversion cycle — allow it to undercut global peers by 30–40% on pricing while maintaining positive gross and net margins. Over the past five years, BYD has achieved a ~53% CAGR in vehicle sales, with exports rising sharply and now accounting for over 23% of total volumes, enhancing both ASPs and profitability.

Capital efficiency, driven by interest-free supplier credit, materially reduces BYD's cost of capital and supports free cash flow generation. However, reliance on a negative cash conversion cycle introduces liquidity considerations: analysts have highlighted potential risks if BYD cannot gradually transition toward a more balanced capital cycle as the business scales globally.

Global EV manufacturing economics are increasingly defined by access to battery materials. European OEMs have had to temporarily shut down factories due to supply shortages. BYD benefits from China's dominant position in lithium, manganese, nickel, and other critical EV inputs, providing supply resilience that non-Chinese OEMs can only hardly replicate.

Our five-year FCFF model, incorporating working capital benefits and conservative terminal assumptions, values BYD at a base case of HKD 111 per share (bull case 128, bear case 105). The valuation reflects both the quality premium of vertical integration, cost leadership, and supply chain resilience, as well as a discount to peers such as Tesla to account for lower exposure to software and AI monetisation.

Business Model & Revenue Drivers

BYD's growth over the past five years has been driven primarily by volume expansion, supported by a balanced BEV/PHEV portfolio. Vehicle sales increased from ~1.5 million units in 2022 to ~4.5 million units in 2025, implying a three-year CAGR of approximately 53%. Historically, PHEVs acted as a transition product in China and delivered slightly higher margins. As of 2025, the mix has shifted toward near parity between BEVs (49.6%) and PHEVs (50.4%), reflecting improved BEV economics and stronger BEV demand in overseas markets.

Geographic mix shift. BYD remains China-centric, but exports have experienced a strong increase — export volumes grew from ~50k units in 2022 to over 1.0 million in 2025, overtaking Tesla's export volume. Export share rose from ~0% to over 23% of total vehicle sales. Key regions: Europe, Latin America, ASEAN.

This matters economically. Overseas units typically carry 30–50% higher ASPs than domestic Chinese sales and operate in more rational pricing environments. Local manufacturing expansion through factory construction in Hungary, Turkey, Brazil, and Southeast Asia further improves export profitability by averting EU tariffs and reducing logistics costs. In ASEAN, BYD has established market leadership in Thailand, accounting for approximately 40% of EV registrations. Europe represents a fast-growing EV market targeting 100% electric by 2035 — BYD's European registrations grew over 200% year-on-year.

Unit Economics & Cost Structure

Batteries account for roughly 30–40% of total NEV production cost, making battery economics the dominant driver of pricing power. BYD's proprietary Blade Battery, based on LFP chemistry, offers lower raw-material intensity, higher thermal safety, and longer cycle life. As a result, BYD's battery pack costs are estimated to be ~29% lower than peers, reinforced by China's broader battery supply chain, where lithium-ion pack prices are materially lower than in North America and Europe.

System-level integration. Beyond chemistry, BYD benefits from platform-level engineering choices such as Cell-to-Body architecture, which integrates the battery directly into the vehicle structure. Teardown analyses (BYD Seal vs Volkswagen ID.3) show cost savings not only in batteries, but also across body, chassis, electronics, labour, and factory overhead. This indicates system-level efficiency, not isolated component savings.

These structural advantages allow BYD to price vehicles 30–40% below global peers while maintaining positive gross and net margins. Despite blended ASPs declining from ~RMB 175k in 2022 to ~RMB 145k in 2024, gross margins expanded to ~19.4%, highlighting the depth of the cost moat. BYD prioritises aggregate cash flow and scale over per-unit margin maximisation.

Capital Efficiency & Working Capital

BYD's most striking advantage is its negative cash conversion cycle. In 2024: CCC of –31.7 days; trade payables form the majority of liabilities; short-term borrowing represents only a small fraction. This means BYD receives cash from customers well before paying suppliers, effectively using interest-free supplier credit to fund R&D and global expansion.

What appears superficially as a low current ratio or high D/E ratio is, in reality, a deliberate financing strategy tied to scale and bargaining power. This dynamic materially lowers BYD's effective cost of capital and enhances free cash flow durability.

Blade Battery & China's Strategic Positioning

Batteries account for 30–40% of total vehicle production cost and therefore represent the most important factor of NEV unit economics. The dominant battery chemistries used by NEV manufacturers are Nickel-Manganese-Cobalt (NMC) for high energy density and Lithium Iron Phosphate (LFP) for cost and safety. NMC batteries enable longer ranges in a compact form factor but rely on nickel and cobalt, while LFP batteries are more cost-effective, durable, and less resource-intensive.

The essential inputs for LFP, NMC, and NCA batteries — lithium, manganese, nickel, and phosphorus — are precisely where BYD's advantage becomes apparent. China controls the majority of global refining and processing capacity for these resources, even where extraction occurs elsewhere. This control over conversion and refining, rather than raw extraction alone, gives China significant leverage over global NEV supply chains. As a Shenzhen-based national champion, BYD benefits directly from this strategic positioning — even in scenarios of global supply chain disruption or trade wars, BYD can continue largely unaffected.

Valuation Framework

A five-year FCFF model was constructed to translate operating assumptions into intrinsic value. Key features: explicit modelling of working capital benefits from negative CCC; conservative terminal assumptions; margin normalisation driven by export mix, not domestic pricing relief.

Cost of Equity (9.76%) was based on a 1.85% Risk-Free Rate (China 10Y) and a 1.5 Beta. The elevated Beta reflects the Country Risk Premium and the volatility of the ongoing EU anti-subsidy narrative. Cost of Debt (2.01%) reflects the current yields on Chinese medium-term corporate notes.

Risks & Considerations

BYD faces several potential challenges that could affect the timing and stability of its margins. Prolonged domestic price competition or geopolitical tensions affecting export markets could pressure profitability. Raw material volatility, particularly for battery inputs, could impact production costs. Execution risks remain in scaling overseas manufacturing operations.

Moreover, BYD's reliance on a negative cash conversion cycle introduces liquidity considerations. While the approach enables efficient funding of growth and global expansion, analysts caution that it may mask rising debt levels and create stress if supplier credit conditions tighten or operational flexibility is reduced. These factors do not negate BYD's structural cost advantages, but they highlight the importance of monitoring capital cycle dynamics as the company continues to scale globally.
